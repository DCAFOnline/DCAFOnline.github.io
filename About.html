About DCAF

Background

The provision of digital services often involves exchanging data between ICT systems (cloud included) and hence implies interoperability between these systems. Digital interoperability is a broader concept than system to system integration. It also encompasses organisations' capability to work collaboratively towards mutually beneficial and commonly agreed goals. In most cases a digital interoperability framework is introduced as a set of policies, standards and guidelines to describe the way in which organisations have agreed, or should agree, to interact with each other.

Why DCAF

There is little doubt that standards or interoperability frameworks are fundamental to enabling digital interoperability. Ensuring they are used in a prescribed way, through governance or other mechanisms , is more important. Governance is the glue that makes interoperability work. Given the diverse range of digital standards in use, there is inevitably going to be gaps in assessing digital service providers' adherence to an interoperability framework or organised collection of standards. Some of these gaps include:

  • The usual absence of a single certification, regulation or suitable compliance regime that governs the use of a framework or suite of standards. Such compliance generally adds more cost and complexity;
  • A lack of an authoritative governance body on cross industry initiatives led by informal multi-stakeholder forums;
  • The unusual practice for a government authority to provide accreditation or certification to private sector organisations for the use of standards or frameworks that do not fall within its regulatory scope;
  • Formal accreditation as only a snapshot at the time it is granted. Unless there are guarantees that software environments do not change and there is a practical way to prove this, the accreditation can only be taken on faith. Data relevant to the accreditation has a tendency to get outdated and needs to be periodically reviewed and updated, often resulting in manually intensive work; and
  • Organisations that publish standards or frameworks and also provide certification of compliance (accreditation) run the risk of transgressing anti-competitive regulations or laws.

These gaps, if left unaddressed, can be expected to significantly inhibit market adoption. There needs to be a simple and cost-effective way for businesses to determine or evaluate whether their providers support an organised collection of standards (or frameworks) and for service providers to voluntarily and transparently publish their 'accreditation' status as a set of digital capability assertions.

The DCAF approach uses transparency as a governance and compliance tool to help facilitate and enable self-regulation of a digitally interoperable ecosystem. Transparency will hold participants to the highest level of service and business conduct.

DCAF is also a a good foundation on which to add specific requirements to cater for particular regulatory regimes.
